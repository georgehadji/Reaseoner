## The Clarity of Simple Words

Good writing starts with simple words. William Zinsser advocated for short, direct verbs and nouns drawn from Anglo-Saxon roots [Source: https://www.scribd.com/document/154897544/Five-Principles-of-Good-Writing]. He urged writers to steer clear of Latinate words, which he found to be lengthy and pretentious [Source: https://www.scribd.com/document/154897544/Five-Principles-of-Good-Writing]. This emphasis on plain language taps into Zinsser’s core principles of clarity, brevity, and simplicity, four elements he identified as fundamental to good writing [Source: https://readabilityformulas.com/the-art-of-simplicity-a-writers-guide/]. Achieving such simplicity is not accidental; it is forged through diligent effort and a clear grasp of one’s thoughts [Source: https://www.gravitasinv.com/reading-book/on-writing-well-william-zinsser/]. The aim is to present ideas plainly, without unnecessary ornamentation, so the reader can grasp the subject without distraction.

## Brevity as a Virtue

Brevity is a virtue in writing, a principle William Zinsser championed [Source: https://www.academia.edu/32037611/Five_Principles-of-Good-Writing]. His approach to authorship consistently prioritized concise expression, alongside clarity, simplicity, and humanity [Source: https://readabilityformulas.com/the-art-of-simplicity-a-writers-guide, Source: https://nysba.org/thoughts-on-legal-writing-from-the-greatest-of-them-all-william-zinsser]. Zinsser believed that effective writing should aim for one clear thought per sentence [Source: https://www.scribd.com/document/154897544/Five-Principles-of-Good-Writing]. This commitment to conciseness means stripping away unnecessary words and phrases. It demands that each word carry its weight, contributing directly to the overall meaning. Applying this to the subject of war in Iran requires a deliberate effort to distill complex events into understandable terms. Every sentence must serve a purpose, avoiding linguistic ornamentation that can obscure rather than enlighten. The aim is not simply to be short, but to be effective through economical language. This practice, according to Zinsser, makes prose stronger and more engaging for the reader [Source: https://www.academia.edu/32037611/Five_Principles-of-Good-Writing].

## Humanity in Reporting

Good reporting, like all good writing, must be rooted in humanity. William Zinsser believed that writers are fundamentally in the storytelling business [Source: jerz.setonhill.edu/blog/2017/11/06/william-zinsser-what-is-good-writing-clarity-simplicity-brevity-and-humanity/]. This perspective implies that even in the face of conflict, the human element must remain central. Zinsser's principles, including clarity, simplicity, brevity, and logic, all serve to connect the writer and reader on a human level [Source: www.scribd.com/document/154897544/Five-Principles-of-Good-Writing]. 

He argued that writing should strive for a conversational tone, particularly as media landscapes evolve [Source: www.academia.edu/32037611/Five_Principles-of-Good-Writing]. This conversational quality is a direct conduit to humanity. When reporting on events, especially those involving human struggle, the aim is to communicate with a reader, not simply to present facts. The reportage must resonate with human experience. Zinsser’s emphasis on clarity and simplicity ensures that stories are accessible, allowing the human aspects of the narrative to shine through without unnecessary obfuscation. Brevity, too, contributes to this, focusing the reader's attention on the core human drama at play, rather than allowing it to be diluted. Logical coherence ensures that the human stories presented are understandable and follow a discernible path, much like a shared conversation. The principles of brevity, humanity, and logical coherence are intertwined in effective reporting [Source: www.academia.edu/32037611/Five_Principles-of-Good-Writing].
